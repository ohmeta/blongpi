# blongpi - Detailed Comparative Genomics Strategy & Technical Record

This document provides an exhaustive technical and biological record of the development of the **blongpi** pipeline. It bridges the gap between the initial 16S-based observations and the genome-resolved comparative analysis of 28 *Bifidobacterium longum* MAGs.

---

## 1. The ASV-MAG Integration Dilemma: A Root Cause Analysis

### 1.1 The "5 ASV" Bottleneck (Technical)
During our exploration of the `b_longum_detailed_analysis.tsv`, we observed that only 5 shared ASVs from your target list successfully matched the 28 MAGs. This is a common challenge in metagenomics known as the **"16S Binning Gap"**:
- **Conserved Sequences**: 16S rRNA genes are highly conserved. Assembly algorithms often collapse multiple strains into a single 16S contig.
- **Repeat Sequences**: Most *Bifidobacterium* species have 2–5 copies of the 16S operon. These repetitive regions frequently cause assembly breaks, leaving the 16S gene on a short, unassigned contig.
- **Binning Logic**: Tools like MetaBat2 use tetranucleotide frequency and coverage. Because 16S genes have highly conserved (different) signatures compared to the rest of the genome, they are often excluded from the MAG bins.
- **Implication**: The "missing" 23 MAGs are likely correct *B. longum* genomes, but they simply lack the 16S "anchor" to link them back to your ASVs.

### 1.2 The ASV_3 Taxonomic Conflict
**Observation**: ASV_3 (RDP: *B. longum*) matched a MAG (GTDB: *B. dentium*) at 95.1% identity.
- **Biological Thresholds**: 
    - **95% Identity**: Typically represents **Genus-level** similarity.
    - **97% Identity**: Represents **Species-level** similarity.
    - **99%+ Identity**: Represents **Strain-level** similarity.
- **Resolution**: Since 95% is below the species threshold, RDP and GTDB are both "correct" in their own context, but GTDB (using ~120 marker genes) is far more accurate. ASV_3 is a 16S sequence that is *related* to both species but is closer to the *B. longum* 16S database sequence, while the MAG's entire genome structure clearly identifies it as *B. dentium*.

## 2. Biological Hypotheses: What Drives "Sharing Efficiency"?

We identified three primary biological mechanisms that could explain why *B. longum* bridges the nasal and gut environments more efficiently in the Exposure group:

### Hypothesis A: The "Bridger" Adhesion Phenotype
Strains in the Exposure group may possess superior machinery for mucosal attachment.
- **Markers**: *Tad (Tight Adherence) pili* (PF01391), *Sortase-dependent pili* (PF00440).
- **Mechanism**: These pili allow the bacteria to form stable micro-colonies in the nasal vestibule and survive the transit to the gut.

### Hypothesis B: Human Milk Oligosaccharide (HMO) Dominance
Exposure strains may be "metabolic specialists" that thrive on infant-specific nutrients.
- **Markers**: *Fucosidases* (PF02837), *Sialidases* (PF01301), *LNB-phosphorylase (lnpA)*.
- **Mechanism**: Higher metabolic efficiency on breast milk leads to higher absolute abundance. Higher abundance increases the probability of translocation between body sites.

### Hypothesis C: Environmental Stress Plasticity
The path from Nose → Mouth → Stomach → Gut is hazardous.
- **Markers**: *Bile Salt Hydrolase (BSH)* (PF00122), *F1F0-ATPase (acid stress)*.
- **Mechanism**: Strains with these genes survive stomach acid and bile salts better, ensuring that "shared" individuals successfully seed the gut.

## 3. Detailed Pipeline Architecture

The **blongpi** project uses a modular design to maximize reliability:

### Step 1: Annotation & Pangenome (Foundation)
- **Tool**: **Prokka** provides rapid, standardized feature calls.
- **Tool**: **Panaroo** is chosen over Roary because it is designed specifically for MAGs. It can "repair" fragmented assemblies and account for missing genes using a graph-based approach, which is critical when working with 28 potentially incomplete bins.
- **Tool**: **IQ-TREE** uses the core-gene alignment to produce a maximum-likelihood tree with 1000 ultrafast bootstraps.

### Step 2: Targeted Functional HMM Search
Instead of relying on general annotations, we use **HMMER** to look for specific protein domains (PFAMs) related to our hypotheses. 
- **Database**: We encourage the user to build a `markers.hmm` file containing the PFAMs listed in Section 2.

### Step 3: Statistical Integration in R
- **Strategy**: We perform a **Fisher's Exact Test** on the Gene Presence/Absence (PA) matrix generated by Panaroo.
- **Input**: The binary PA matrix + the Metadata (Control vs Exposure).
- **Output**: A list of "Exposure-Enriched Genes" which are the candidate drivers of the sharing phenotype.

## 4. How to Interpret the Final Results

Once the pipeline completes, the **`blongpi/results/`** folder will contain:
1.  **`blongum_final_report.pdf`**:
    - **Top**: A phylogenetic tree. If Exposure-group MAGs cluster together, it indicates a clonal transmission event.
    - **Bottom**: A heatmap of key genes. If the "Exposure" columns are mostly blue (present) for `tadA` or `bsh` while "Control" is white (absent), Hypothesis A or C is supported.
2.  **`enriched_genes.tsv`**: Look for genes with `adj_p < 0.05`. These are the genomic "smoking guns" that distinguish your exposure infants' *B. longum* from the controls.
